PRACTICE ECONOMICS

The Economics of a
GLP Peptide Program:
What the Numbers Actually Look Like

Majestic R&D Editorial April 24, 2026 7 min read Practice Business

Most physicians evaluating a compounded GLP peptide program arrive at the conversation without a clear picture of what the underlying economics actually look like. The marketing language — "revenue from day one," "$60K year-one potential" — is directionally accurate but abstracted. What's missing is the cost structure: where the money comes from, where it goes, what the margins are on each compound, and how the financial model holds up at different patient volumes.

This article lays out the economics of a compounded GLP peptide program in plain terms. No sales pitch. Just the cost structure, the margin math, and an honest assessment of where the model is strong and where the variables matter.

The cost structure: what you're actually buying

A starter package in a compounded GLP program is fundamentally an inventory purchase. The practice buys a quantity of compounded peptide — typically structured around a $30,000 initial order — and resells it to patients as part of a clinical program. The economics are those of a product-based revenue stream layered on top of a service-based practice.

The three relevant cost categories are:

  • Inventory cost: The wholesale cost of the compounded peptide per unit (vial). This is the primary cost of goods. At Majestic published pricing: Semaglutide at $262.50 per 30mg vial, Tirzepatide at $750.00 per 200mg vial, Retatrutide at $525.00 per 60mg vial.
  • Financing cost: If the practice uses PNC Healthcare financing for the starter order, the carrying cost is approximately $650/month over 60 months at 10% APR on a $30,000 draw. This is the baseline overhead the program must cover before it generates net revenue.
  • Program overhead: Staff time for patient management, compliance documentation, storage, and administration. In established practices with efficient workflows, this is largely absorbed into existing overhead. In practices building the program from scratch, there is a startup cost in staff time during the first 30–60 days.

The key insight on cost structure: Unlike most clinical services, the primary cost in a peptide program is inventory — a tangible asset with a shelf life, not a sunk cost. Inventory that moves quickly generates revenue. Inventory that doesn't move is capital tied up in product. The financial health of the program is therefore directly tied to patient volume and inventory velocity — how quickly the practice cycles through its initial order.

The three compounds have meaningfully different margin profiles, driven by their cost per mg and the volume of compound required per patient per month. Understanding this matters for how a practice thinks about its compound mix.

This is worth understanding when evaluating compound mix, even though the clinical decision of which compound to use belongs entirely to the physician.

The inventory velocity question

The single most important variable in the financial model is how quickly the practice burns through its initial inventory. This is not simply a function of patient count — it is a function of patient count multiplied by compound mix and average monthly usage per patient.

A practice burning through a $30,000 inventory order in six months is cycling capital efficiently. Revenue exceeds the financing payment early, reorders build inventory depth, and the program develops momentum. A practice taking 18 months to move the same inventory has a different problem: carrying cost is accumulating against a slow-moving asset, and the program's financial case weakens with each passing month.

Where the revenue actually comes from

It is worth being precise about the revenue model, because it is different from most clinical revenue streams physicians are accustomed to.

Insurance-billed services generate revenue through a coding and reimbursement structure that is largely outside the physician's direct control. The practice bills, the payer adjudicates, and the practice collects some fraction of what it billed, on a timeline that can stretch weeks or months.

A cash-pay GLP peptide program operates on a fundamentally different model. The practice purchases inventory at wholesale cost and provides it to patients as part of a clinical program at a markup that reflects the value delivered. Revenue is collected at the point of service. There is no claims process, no adjudication, no 45-day collections cycle. The cash-pay structure is what makes the program's economics as clean as they are — and it is also what makes the patient volume variable so directly impactful on cash flow.

The compounding effect of retention: A patient who continues their program for 12 months generates substantially more revenue than the initial month implies. The startup cost of acquiring and onboarding a patient — intake, documentation, first visit — is a one-time cost. The monthly revenue from that patient is recurring. Programs with strong patient retention at months 3, 6, and 12 have dramatically better economics than programs with high early attrition, even at identical initial patient volumes.

The financial case at different practice sizes

The economics look different depending on the size and type of practice. A few honest observations:

Small primary care or family medicine practices (under 500 active patients)

The financial case is viable but depends heavily on identifying and converting the right subset of existing patients. The program's economics are tight at low patient volumes — the $650/month financing payment requires a minimum patient base to cover comfortably. The Metis Profit Snapshot is particularly valuable here: knowing your specific patient population's likely conversion rate before committing to the starter order is exactly the kind of information that makes the decision defensible.

Mid-size practices (500–2,000 active patients)

This is where the program's economics are most straightforward. A practice with 1,000 active patients likely has 80–150 individuals who meet a reasonable metabolic profile for a GLP program. Converting even 20–30 of those patients in the first 90 days produces inventory velocity and revenue that comfortably exceeds the financing overhead. This is the core target demographic for the program.

Weight loss clinics and metabolic medicine practices

For practices already focused on metabolic health, the program is effectively an inventory optimization play. The patient base is already pre-selected. The question is cost of goods and supply chain reliability, not patient conversion. These practices typically move inventory fastest and have the most mature financial picture from the program earliest.

Med spas and aesthetics practices

The cash-pay infrastructure is already built. Patients are already accustomed to paying out of pocket for clinical services. The marginal cost of adding a GLP program in terms of patient education and billing infrastructure is low. The constraint is typically physician or NP oversight requirements depending on the state, not the economics themselves.

What the model does not account for

An honest financial overview has to name what the projections don't include:

  • Patient attrition: Some patients discontinue within 90 days — cost intolerance, side effects, life circumstances. Programs that don't plan for attrition and build patient pipelines accordingly will find their inventory burn rate slowing at the 3-month mark.
  • Regulatory changes: The compounded GLP-1 market has been subject to FDA scrutiny related to shortage list status. Changes to the regulatory landscape could affect the availability or legal basis for compounded GLP-1s. Practices should stay current on FDA guidance and work with compliant suppliers whose documentation is audit-ready.
  • Competition: The compounded GLP market is competitive and getting more so. Practices that establish programs now benefit from first-mover advantage in their patient population, but pricing pressure from direct-to-consumer telehealth platforms is a real market dynamic.
  • Staff time in year one: The first 60–90 days of a new program require disproportionate staff attention. Intake workflows, documentation templates, and patient communication protocols that feel efficient by month four are not efficient on day one. Factor this in honestly.

The bottom line: The financial case for a well-run GLP peptide program at appropriate patient volumes is sound. The economics are not complicated — they are a function of wholesale cost, inventory velocity, and patient retention. The practices that perform best financially are those that approach the program with the same operational discipline they apply to any other revenue line: clear intake processes, consistent patient follow-through, and a realistic picture of the patient population before the first order is placed.
