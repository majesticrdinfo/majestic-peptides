PRACTICE GROWTH

Why Most Practices
Undercount Their
GLP-Eligible Patients

Majestic R&D Editorial April 24, 2026 6 min read Practice Business

When physicians evaluate whether a GLP peptide program makes financial sense for their practice, the first question they ask is usually about economics: cost, margin, financing. The question they should be asking first is simpler and more fundamental: how many patients in my existing panel would benefit from this kind of program?

In almost every case, the honest answer is: more than you think. Not because physicians are inattentive — but because the structural realities of how practices document metabolic conditions, how obesity is coded and tracked in EHR systems, and how patient conversations about weight management typically unfold all systematically produce an undercount. Physicians who do a rigorous chart review before evaluating the program almost universally come back with a larger eligible population than their initial estimate.

This article explains why the undercount happens and what a systematic approach to identifying the actual eligible population looks like.

Why the intuitive estimate is almost always too low

Ask a physician with 1,200 active patients how many would be candidates for a GLP metabolic program and a common answer is somewhere in the range of 20–40. A rigorous EHR query of the same panel typically surfaces 100–200. The gap is not a failure of clinical awareness — it is the predictable result of how patients with metabolic conditions are actually documented and how physician memory of their panel works.

Mental availability bias

Physician estimates of patient population characteristics are heavily influenced by which patients are most mentally available — meaning patients seen recently, patients with complex presentations, or patients who have explicitly raised the topic. The overweight patient who came in last week for a knee complaint and never mentioned weight management does not feature in a physician's mental model of their "GLP candidates," even though they may be an excellent candidate on paper.

Documentation gaps in obesity coding

Obesity is systematically under-coded in outpatient EHRs. Studies consistently show that when a patient's BMI is documented and clearly falls in the obese range, an obesity ICD-10 code (E66.xx) appears in the problem list or encounter diagnosis far less often than the BMI data alone would suggest. The patient's metabolic profile exists in the chart — in vital signs, lab values, and comorbidity codes — but is not consolidated into a searchable obesity diagnosis.

This means a query for "patients with an obesity diagnosis" will miss a significant fraction of patients who are objectively obese by BMI criteria. The eligible population is larger than the coded population.

The comorbidity door is often more reliable than the BMI door

Patients are more reliably coded for the downstream consequences of excess weight than for the weight condition itself. Type 2 diabetes, hypertension, hyperlipidemia, sleep apnea, NAFLD/MASLD, and osteoarthritis are all conditions with strong metabolic overlap with the population that benefits most from a GLP program — and all are more consistently documented in EHR problem lists than obesity alone.

The implication for chart review: A search strategy that begins with BMI thresholds and comorbidity codes in combination will surface a substantially larger and more accurate eligible population than a search for obesity diagnosis codes alone. The patients are in the chart. The data is there. It is a matter of querying for it systematically rather than relying on mental availability or ICD-10 coding consistency.

The ICD-10 codes that identify the population

A systematic EHR query for GLP-eligible patients uses metabolic and weight-related ICD-10 codes in combination rather than relying on any single code. The relevant code families are:

E66.xx — Obesity
E66.01 (morbid), E66.09 (other), E66.1 (drug-induced), E66.9 (unspecified). Query all E66 subcodes, not just E66.01 — most patients coded at all are coded at E66.09 or E66.9.

BMI as the anchor, comorbidities as the filter

The most reliable query strategy uses BMI data — which is documented in nearly every EHR from vital sign capture — as the primary filter, then layers comorbidity codes to segment the population by clinical profile.

A practice with 1,000 active patients running this query should expect to find somewhere between 80 and 150 patients who meet the BMI threshold and have at least one relevant comorbidity. The exact number depends on the practice's patient demographics, specialty focus, and geographic population characteristics. Practices in higher-BMI demographic areas or with a chronic disease management focus will be at the high end. Pediatric-heavy or acute-care-focused practices will be at the low end.

The lapsed patient population: the most overlooked segment

Active panel counts understate the opportunity. Most practices have a meaningful population of lapsed patients — patients who were seen within the past two to three years but have not had a visit recently. These patients are often lapsed precisely because they have chronic conditions that have not been adequately addressed, weight management among them.

A query that extends the lookback window to 24–36 months and includes patients with documented metabolic comorbidities who have not had a recent visit often surfaces a significant additional population. These patients are not lost — they are inactive. A practice that launches a GLP program has a concrete reason to re-engage them that is clinical rather than administrative.

What the query does not do

A chart review query identifies the population of patients who meet objective criteria for potential eligibility. It does not make clinical decisions. Every patient identified through this process requires individual clinical evaluation — the physician reviews the patient's full history, contraindications, current medications, and clinical goals before any clinical recommendation is made.

The purpose of the query is to give the physician an accurate picture of the population size, not to generate a prescribing list. It answers the question: "How many patients in my practice are the kind of patients this program is designed for?" — which is exactly the information needed to make an informed decision about whether to launch the program at all.

The summary case: The patients who would benefit from a GLP metabolic program are almost certainly already in your practice in larger numbers than your intuitive estimate suggests. The gap between the mental estimate and the actual eligible population is a structural artifact of how EHR documentation works and how physician memory of a panel operates — not a reflection of reality. A 20-minute systematic query closes that gap and gives you an honest foundation for evaluating the program's financial case for your specific practice.
